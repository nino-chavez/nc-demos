The portal feels off because it's organized by the producer's taxonomy, not the reader's questions. The current IA mirrors Blueprint's pipeline — research, decisions, prototype, validation — which is how the work was *made*, not what anyone landing there needs. That's the org-chart-navigation failure, and no amount of iterating on section copy fixes a wrong axis. So yes: redefine, don't iterate.

**The actual JTBD for film-room.** Who lands there: you in three months, maybe Travis or a 630 coach, and an agent resuming a session. Every one of them arrives with the same first question — "can this process my clips, and what do I do to get clips in and results out?" — plus a second one, "what state is it in right now?" Nobody arrives asking "what did Stage 1 research conclude?" Film-room's portal reads off because it's showcasing the methodology instead of the product. The methodology is the scaffolding; the reader came for the building.

**Why the persona switcher is fluff here.** It's solving a real problem (readers need different depths) with the wrong mechanism (parallel content tracks). Three tracks triple authoring cost, so each track ends up thin, and film-room doesn't even have three audiences — it has one audience at different depths. The switcher made sense in the self-application portal, where consumers/contributors/stakeholders genuinely diverge; it got stamped into consumers as if that were universal. It isn't.

**Why the ADRs read as internal speak.** Because they are. An ADR is written at the decision moment, agent-to-operator, and that's correct — don't rewrite the ADRs. The error is surfacing them raw as portal content. The portal should show decision *cards* — one line: what was decided, what it means for the thing you're using — linking to the full ADR for anyone who hits the wall that decision explains. Translate at the surface, keep the artifact internal-voiced.

**Three candidate IAs, compared against the real initiatives** (per the design-decision rule — these need to be developed enough to compare, not one draft to tear apart):

- **A — Task-oriented**: sections are reader verbs. *What this is / See it work / Run it / Current state / Why it's shaped this way*. Fits film-room perfectly (a tool with a demo and run instructions). Fits rally-hq. Strains for the self-application, where "run it" isn't the job — "adopt it" is.
- **B — Single-brief dossier**: one page. Status brief up top, demo, evidence inline, artifact archive as an appendix. No nav taxonomy at all. Cheapest to author, strongest for solo initiatives, but collapses for anything with real stakeholder review cycles — rally-hq feedback triage needs addressable sections.
- **C — Depth-layered**: three layers by engagement, not persona. Layer 1: the pitch/demo, 30 seconds. Layer 2: current state + the decisions that matter, 5 minutes. Layer 3: raw artifact archive (research, full ADRs, validation) — for agents and deep-divers. Personas dissolve into depth. One content track, progressive disclosure.

C wins, with B's instinct folded in: layer 1+2 *is* a dossier, layer 3 is the archive the methodology already produces for free. It handles film-room (mostly layers 1–2), rally-hq (all three), and the self-application (layer 3 is heaviest there, which is correct — its readers are adopters). A's verb sections become layer-2 headings rather than the top-level axis, because "current state" and "why" are questions every initiative shares while "run it" isn't.

The template change this implies: `blueprint.yml portal:` declares the audience shape, and the stamper collapses layers accordingly — a solo tool initiative gets a two-layer portal by default, no switcher, decision cards instead of ADR listings. That's a methodology change (`template/`), so it should land as a wave with an ADR of its own, and film-room becomes the first consumer to prove it.

That's my read. If you want, next step is drafting the layer-2 "decision card" shape against film-room's actual ADRs to pressure-test C before touching the template.
