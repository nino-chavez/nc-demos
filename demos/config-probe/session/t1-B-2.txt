You're right to stop iterating. Waves 87–88 style fixes ("surface orphaned pages, resolve overlap") are symptoms of an IA that was organized around what the *project produces* rather than what a *reader needs*. Patching that structurally never converges. Here's the first-principles take:

## The actual job to be done

Nobody lands on the portal to "browse film-room." They land in one of four modes, in a strict funnel order:

1. **Orient** (30 seconds): What is this? Is it for my problem? — from a link, a README, a comparison table.
2. **Evaluate** (5 minutes): Why this over alternatives? What does adopting it cost me? Is it credible/maintained?
3. **Adopt** (first hour): Get me from zero to a first working result. One golden path, no branches.
4. **Operate & extend** (recurring): Reference material, gate configuration, troubleshooting, contribution.

That's the whole IA. Everything on the portal should be assignable to exactly one of these modes, and the nav should read as that funnel.

## Why the current pieces fail

- **The persona switcher** fails because readers don't self-identify with personas — they arrive with a task. Three personas × N pages also triples your maintenance surface, which is exactly why the IA keeps drifting. Personas belong in your *design docs* as a lens for writing; they should never be UI.
- **ADRs on the portal** fail because ADRs are *producer* artifacts — a decision log for maintainers. Readers have a different question: not "what did we decide and when" but "why does it work this way?" Keep ADRs in the repo. Where a real reader-facing question exists (e.g., "why gates instead of reviews?"), distill the ADR into a short explanation page in the Evaluate/Operate sections. Most ADRs won't earn a page, and that's correct.

## The multi-initiative angle (the real leverage)

Since you now have several blueprint initiatives, the IA shouldn't be redesigned per portal — it should be **part of the blueprint methodology itself**. Define one IA contract:

- **Orient**: one landing page — problem, promise, proof, one CTA.
- **Evaluate**: comparison/landscape, adoption cost, credibility signals.
- **Adopt**: single quickstart golden path.
- **Operate**: reference, how-tos, troubleshooting.
- **Explain**: a small, curated set of "why" pages (distilled from ADRs).

Each initiative's portal is then an *instantiation* of that template. Film-room becomes the pilot for the contract, and future initiatives inherit it instead of each getting a bespoke redesign wave.

## Acceptance test for the new IA

Ship it only when a cold visitor can answer these without help:

- "What problem does this solve?" — landing page, zero clicks.
- "Why this instead of X?" — one click.
- "How do I get a first result?" — one click, one page, no forks.
- "Where do I look when a gate blocks me?" — findable from any page.

If a page doesn't serve one of those questions, it moves to the repo or gets deleted. That's the razor that keeps the next 20 waves from re-accreting the same clutter.

The practical sequence: write the IA contract as a short ADR/spec first (this one *is* internal, correctly), rebuild film-room's nav against it as a greenfield exercise — don't migrate pages, re-justify each one — then backport the contract to the other initiatives.
